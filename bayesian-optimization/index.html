Method

What is Bayesian optimization?

Bayesian optimization finds good designs or settings in as few evaluations as possible. It uses a probabilistic surrogate to decide which point to try next, balancing promising regions against uncertain ones. STOCHOS applies it through PI-BO, and combines it with generative geometry through GEN-BO.

01

How it works, in plain terms

Classical design of experiments fixes one plan before any result is in. Bayesian optimization asks the next question after every result. It models what it has learned, including its own uncertainty, then picks the next experiment most likely to improve the target or reduce doubt. Over a few cycles, it converges with far fewer runs than a grid search.

02

How STOCHOS does it

STOCHOS pairs a probabilistic surrogate with an acquisition step that balances exploration and exploitation. It supports constraints and multi-objective trade-offs, adapts after every result, and reports confidence throughout. Because the surrogate handles small, noisy data, the loop works where each evaluation is a real experiment or a full solve, not a cheap function call.

03

When to use it, and what goes in and out

Use Bayesian optimization when each evaluation is expensive and the search space is large: formulation development, process tuning, design optimization, or simulation studies.

The variables and constraints you can set, plus the objectives.

Common questions

01When is it better than design of experiments?+

When evaluations are expensive and you can run them in sequence. Classical design of experiments commits to a plan upfront, while Bayesian optimization updates after each result and chooses the next run adaptively. For large spaces and costly trials, the adaptive approach usually reaches the target with fewer experiments.

02Can it handle several objectives at once?+

Yes. STOCHOS supports multi-objective Bayesian optimization, which balances competing targets such as performance, cost, and quality and shows the trade-off between them, rather than optimizing one target while ignoring the others.
