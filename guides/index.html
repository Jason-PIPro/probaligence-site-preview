Guides

Guides for the problem you already have.

Ten articles that start from a difficulty you recognise from your own work, explain the method that addresses it in plain language, and stop where it meets the workflow you already run.

What a guide is

A guide is a problem-first article. It opens on a pain you already feel, works through the method that addresses it, and ends at the point where it meets the tools you already run. The method pages define the terms and stay definitional; the guides go deeper into what happens when you apply them to a real campaign. Each one links down into the method page that owns its definition.

Bayesian optimization for engineers

Testing every combination is not an option. Bayesian optimization chooses each next experiment from the last result, so you reach a better design in tens of runs, not hundreds.

When can you trust an AI prediction?

A prediction without a confidence range is not a decision you can sign off. Uncertainty-aware models report how far to trust each result, and when to go back to the solver.

Build an ML workflow without an ML team

You do not need a data scientist to put machine learning on your simulation data. A visual workflow builds, trains, and deploys the model with the engineer who owns the problem.

Which of your parameters actually matter

Most inputs barely move the result. Sensitivity analysis ranks what actually drives your response, so the next campaign varies the parameters that count.
Engineering

Predict a full field, not a single number

Peak stress does not tell you where it is. Field-capable surrogate models predict the whole distribution across the geometry in seconds, so you keep the spatial answer.
Engineering

Generate design variants that respect your constraints

Manual variant building limits how many designs you ever evaluate. A model learns geometry from your existing designs and generates new ones that keep the features you cannot move.
Chemistry

Cut lab to production scale-up risk

Lab results rarely survive plant scale. Multi-fidelity models combine cheap small-scale data with a few expensive runs to predict plant behaviour before the batch is made.
Chemistry

Keep up with a high-throughput lab

Automation moved the bottleneck from the bench to planning. A model chooses the next plate from the last one, so throughput turns into information instead of volume.
Paint

Match a colour in two batches, not seven

Every correction loop costs a batch. A model trained on your own recipes proposes a closer first match, so you converge in a couple of batches instead of a week of tweaks.
