Method

What is sensitivity analysis?

Sensitivity analysis identifies which inputs most affect an output, and by how much. Where prediction tells you what may happen, sensitivity analysis explains why, and shows where to focus next. STOCHOS computes it with Sobol indices, SHAP, distance correlation, and mutual information, on the same model it uses to predict.

01

What it tells you

Sensitivity analysis turns a prediction into engineering insight. It ranks the variables that drive the outcome globally and explains why a specific design, batch, or operating point behaved as it did. That focuses experiments and design effort on the factors that matter and away from the ones that do not.

02

How STOCHOS does it

STOCHOS runs sensitivity analysis on the trained probabilistic model, so it is fast and uses no extra expensive evaluations. The result is a clear ranking of drivers and interactions.

When to use it, and what goes in and out

Use sensitivity analysis to reduce a large parameter set, explain an unexpected result, or decide which inputs to control tightly.

Inputs

The trained model and its variables.

Outputs

A ranked list of drivers and interactions that guides the next experiments and design changes.

A concrete example

Change the factors that move the target

In formulation work, sensitivity analysis shows which raw materials and process settings drive color, gloss, or viscosity, and which interactions matter. Instead of adjusting ingredients by trial and error, a developer sees the cause structure and changes the factors that move the target.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Sobol indices versus SHAP?+

Sobol indices attribute the variance of an output to inputs globally, across the whole space. SHAP explains a single prediction by sharing its value among the inputs. STOCHOS supports both: Sobol for "what drives the result overall" and SHAP for "why did this specific case behave this way."

Does it need extra experiments?+

No. STOCHOS computes sensitivity on the trained surrogate model, so it reuses the data already gathered and adds no expensive runs. The analysis then guides where new experiments would be most useful.
